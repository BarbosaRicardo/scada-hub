\setlist[enumerate]{noitemsep, topsep=2pt, leftmargin=*, label=\textcolor{mblue}{\small\bfseries\arabic*.}}
\begin{enumerate}\small
  \item \textbf{Introduction} --- Modbus was written in 1979 and has not changed its core protocol since. This chapter establishes why that matters: the same register model running in 1980s PLCs ships unchanged in new devices today. Engineers who understand the history stop treating its quirks as bugs.
  \item \textbf{Data Model} --- Four register tables --- coil, discrete input, input register, holding register --- define every possible Modbus transaction. This chapter makes the address space automatic before function codes are introduced.
  \item \textbf{RTU Mode} --- Serial framing, CRC-16, RS-485 multi-drop wiring, and the silence intervals that separate frames. RTU is still the dominant implementation in field devices; engineers who skip this chapter struggle with every serial deployment.
  \item \textbf{ASCII Mode} --- The human-readable serial variant: twice the overhead of RTU for the same data. Covered because legacy systems still use it, and because understanding it clarifies exactly why RTU won.
  \item \textbf{Modbus TCP} --- MBAP header, transaction IDs, port 502, and how Modbus crossed to Ethernet without modifying its register model. This is why Modbus remains relevant on modern plant networks.
  \item \textbf{Function Codes} --- The 20 codes covering 95\% of deployments: read coils, read/write registers, write multiple registers, and the diagnostics codes that only appear when a device is misbehaving.
  \item \textbf{Exception Codes} --- Illegal Function, Illegal Data Address, Illegal Data Value, Slave Device Failure. Engineers learn to read exception responses instead of assuming the master is at fault.
  \item \textbf{Data Types} --- 16-bit registers were not designed for floats, 32-bit integers, or strings. This chapter covers register-pair encoding, byte-order conventions, and the vendor-specific choices that turn a simple read into an hour of debugging.
  \item \textbf{Troubleshooting} --- Addressing offset errors, byte-order mismatches, poll-rate overloads, RS-485 termination faults, and the firewall that silently drops Modbus TCP. Field diagnosis using Wireshark and software masters.
  \item \textbf{Security} --- Modbus has no authentication, no encryption, and no access control by design. This chapter covers compensating controls: network segmentation, unidirectional gateways, and anomaly detection at the network layer.
  \item \textbf{Practice Lab} --- Five diagnostic exercises: mapping an unknown device's registers, tracing an Exception Code 02, configuring an RTU-to-TCP gateway, diagnosing a byte-order fault, and building a Wireshark filter to monitor a Modbus TCP master.
\end{enumerate}
